Here are some quotes from my parents.

> I've been reading this really good book lately, and I learned that you must eat a meal in a particular order: fibers first, then proteins, and finally the staple. So in practice this means vegetables, meat, and finally rice or bread. So when you're eating a sandwich, you have to take it apart and eat it layer by layer.

— Dad, on *Glucose Revolution*.

> No matter what you do, you should learn from this table: never lose your purpose.
>
> Me: TABLES DON'T HAVE PURPOSES. IT'S A TABLE.

— Mom, a little drunk.

> Look at all the students lining up at the bus stop! College students these days. It's just a few minutes' walk and they're still waiting for the bus! So lazy!

— Dad, on the way to his car.

> Your politics grade sucked so bad, yet you still got an A. That just proves the effectiveness of Marxism and Socialism!
>
> Me: That just means everybody's grade sucked.
>
> Yes. My daughter is the best!

— Mom, on my grades.

> When I was pursuing my Masters, I had to solve a problem: to lower the thermal expansion coefficient of an alloy made by dissolving silicon carbide ($SiC$) in molten aluminum. To make the coefficient low enough, the concentration of $SiC$ needs to be high enough, but it's hard to reach such a high concentration. I spent two years trying to figure out how to get this much $SiC$ dissolved in aluminum. My professor was leaving soon, and I didn't have much time left. Until that day, I saw someone throw out a centrifuge machine. It's like a washing machine; it spins stuff. I fished it out of the trash and dragged it back to my lab, thinking, "what if I just spin the tube real hard until all the $SiC$ condenses at the end of the tube?" Now, molten aluminum came at at least 700-800 Celsius. If it spilled out the machine and hit me, even if I survive, I'd be permanently disfigured. So I extended the start button with a cord long enough to reach out the room, put the tube in, ran out of the room, shut the door, and pressed the button. The machine started spinning. After like ten seconds, the aluminum had solidified. I took the tube out of the machine and cut the bottom part out. There I had it. High concentration of dissolved $SiC$, low thermal expansion coefficient. But were the two previous years of my effort a waste? No! They served as a buildup to this point.

— Dad, *completely* unprompted.
